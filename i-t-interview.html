Mark Murray ​

RIS PACS Administrator, Clinical Radiographer ​

On Thursday, the 7th of April, we had the opportunity to interview the PACS Administrator, Mark Murray.
This report covers what it is like working as a PACS Administrator and gaining a deeper view of Mark's early career life, his motivation for exploring technology, and his career path to becoming a PACS administrator. We had a more in-depth conversation regarding the health IT industry. We discussed other industry-related topics such as different interactions, day-to-day tasks, systems, challenges, and what he enjoys the most about his job. Based on our interview with Mark, we have gone more in-depth and researched different career paths, Medical and technical required skills individuals could take to become a PACS administrator.

Our interviewee Mark Murray is a RIS PACS Administrator and a casual radiographer with the Murrumbidgee Local Health District and Southern NSW Local Health District. According to eHealth, NSW Government "Radiology information systems and picture archiving and communication systems (RIS-PACS) are electronic solutions used by medical imaging departments to manage their services. They track a patient's journey from an image request through to diagnosis - storing key information, digital images and results for easy access by treating teams."

Mark discovered his passion for IT very early on in his career as he spent his spare time building and working on computers at home. He started his career as a federally registered health professional and radiographer. His opportunity to make a career in IT came when medical imaging systems were making a transition from film to digital. This period brought on a lot of change and innovation. His organisation needed staff that could get involved in the testing process and conversion of film images to digital files on patient records systems.

The changes allowed Mark to transition into a role as RIS PACS Application Specialist and then onto his current role as RIS PACS Administrator. He was not required to have formal qualifications in IT but has completed many short courses on IT and aims to complete a short course on SQL to develop his skills further. Becoming a RIS PACS Administrator first required one to be a radiographer first, as the role required specialist domain knowledge in medical imaging to understand and resolve the issues that arise in this position.

The role of a RIS PACS Administrator is to support the many systems that allow radiographers and radiologists to deliver medical imaging services to patients. The systems listed by Mark included the Patient Management Medical Records System, Imaging System, Patient Information Management System, and the Electronic Medical Records System. These systems are the backbone of all health services operated around the country.

Mark's day to day activities varies depending on if there are any outages or project work required at the time. A large part of his time is taking calls and answering tickets received at the service desk. Many of the enquiries are related to error correction. An example of this type of enquiry would be if a radiographer has either taken an x-ray of the left side of a limb instead of the right side or if the x-ray image was mislabeled. Another example will be if the x-ray images have been stored under the incorrect patient file. Mark is often the point of contact to have these errors corrected within the RIS PAC System. Another essential part of his role is training radiographers in using the Radiology Information System (RIS). On an ad-hoc basis, he also is required to deal with system outages. However, this type of issue is often escalated to a state-wide IT Service Desk to be resolved by Server or Network specialists.

Different interactions are a big part of Mark's role; as a PACS Administrator, his day-to-day interactions vary from working alongside System users, which are Radiologists, radiographers, and clinical staff; for different issues arising, he also interacts with server administrators and Network Administrators. He also interacts with patients via a patient portal where patients can have easier access to their X-RAYS. Mark mentioned that working in Health IT, there are different experts for different systems; he used an example that if a system error occurs where the system has failed to receive messages, this issue is allocated to the State-wide Service desk by raising a ticket.

For some, being on call 24/7 might be a challenge, but as a PACS administrator, Mark's primary focus is patient care. Being in Health IT for about 30 years every day is not the same as Mark mentioned; every day is a different challenge. As a PACS administrator who covers the Murrumbidgee Local health district and Southern NSW, Mark finds system outages the most challenging as there is a high demand for X Rays from patients. He mentioned that the best way to approach these challenges is to be patient with people and understand their needs during these unexpected outages. Despite the challenges Mark faces every day, he is very passionate and appreciates his work.

To be a PACS Administrator, you will need to hold a degree in radiologic technology or a field related to healthcare, such as healthcare administration. Mark mentioned it is best to have a strong interest in IT as there are different interacting systems that one needs to get familiar with within this role. His role connects to the medical field as it requires prior experience in the medical industry. He mentioned that you would have basic knowledge of how the systems work as a radiographer. However, as a PACS Administrator, your knowledge of the systems goes much deeper to maintain, manage and error control the systems. As a PACS Administrator, it is crucial to gain prior experience, preferably as a radiologist or radiographer.

Based on our research, there are many career paths to becoming a PACS administrator, but some essential skills are required for this role. One of the main requirements is holding a degree in a field related to healthcare, such as a bachelor's degree in medical imaging, radiography, or applied science. The other aspect of a PACS Administrator is the technical skills required to have deep knowledge of the industry, programs, and systems such as Health Level 7 (HL7), Digital imaging and communications (DICOM) in medicine. Mark also mentioned that it is beneficial to learn SQL because it is used in database systems. Besides the technical and medical skills required for this job, communication skills are a crucial point when dealing with patients, vendors, and providers, as they should be able to explain incredibly high technical information. This role requires a niche skillset and a broad number of challenges, but as Mark mentioned, it is one of the best roles he has been in, and he very much enjoys his current role.
